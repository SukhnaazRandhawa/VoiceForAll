Pre-Meeting Report

Since last week, I have finalized my project idea, which is a bidirectional AI-powered
sign language translation system focusing on American Sign Language (ASL) and British 
Sign Language (BSL) using cross-linguistic transfer learning. 
I also conducted a detailed feasibility analysis: I confirmed access 
to the WLASL dataset (21,083 ASL videos covering 2,000 signs), explored BSL data 
sources such as the BSL Corpus and BSL SignBank, and estimated that around 5,000–8,000 
BSL videos covering 500–1,000 signs are usable. This highlighted a significant data 
disparity between ASL and BSL, which I plan to turn into a research opportunity by 
exploring how transfer learning can help bridge this gap.

For tomorrow’s meeting, I would like to discuss my implementation 
strategy and confirm if the proposed staged approach is appropriate. 
My plan is to start with ASL (the high-resource language) to develop a complete 
working system, and then extend to BSL (the low-resource language) using transfer 
learning techniques. This approach ensures that I have a functional prototype early 
on while managing the risk associated with limited data availability.

In terms of research contribution, I intend to focus on the ASL–BSL data imbalance 
as my core research direction. The goal is to demonstrate how models trained on 
high-resource sign languages can support the development of systems for low-resource ones, 
and potentially generalize this method to other underrepresented sign languages worldwide.
Finally, I would like to confirm whether I am free to choose any tech stack for the 
implementation, or if there are any recommended frameworks or technologies that I should 
follow.



Post-Meeting Report

In today’s meeting, I discussed whether I can begin working on my project and 
if there are any specific tech stack requirements. 
It was confirmed that I am free to choose the technologies I prefer for implementation.

We also reviewed the project plan and concluded that I should start with 
ASL (American Sign Language) first, as it has the largest amount of data and 
available resources. If time permits later, I can extend the system to 
include BSL (British Sign Language), as it is a lower-resource language.

Finally, we discussed the project's complexity in relation to the degree 
requirements, and it was agreed that the project has sufficient depth and 
potential complexity to meet the expectations for my program.
