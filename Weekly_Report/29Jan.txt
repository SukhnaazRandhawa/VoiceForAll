Following on from my last update, I have completed the data augmentation phase and conducted extensive real world testing which revealed important findings.
This is what I completed since my last update => 
1. Data Augmentation Pipeline
    * Implemented synthetic data augmentation techniques: horizontal flipping, rotation, scaling, noise addition, and time warping
    * Expanded training dataset from 399 to 3990 samples 
    * After this the accuracy was 96% but when I tested in the real time it was much less. The issue was data leak. I used the same data for the traning and the testing. 
    * I discovered and corrected a data leakage issue: the augmentation must be applied only after splitting train/test to avoid augmented copies of the same video appearing in both sets
2. LSTM Baseline Results 
    * After fixing the data leak the 
    * Test Accuracy=> 57% (random baseline would be 5%)
    * Per-word accuracy varies significantly:
        * Excellent (100%)=> chair, clothes, help
        * Good (71-80%)=>  book, drink, cousin, walk
        * Moderate (50-60%)=> computer, who, year
        * Poor (0-40%)=> no, thin, before, deaf
3. Real-World Webcam Testing Observations I tested the model extensively with my own signing and documented the following limitations:
    * Similar signs cause confusion: Signs with similar hand shapes get misclassified (eg: "no" recognised as "yes", "deaf" as "who", "candy" as "who")
    * Hand occlusion problem: When one hand is behind the other, MediaPipe cannot track the occluded hand, leading to incorrect predictions
    * Static vs motion recognition: The model appears to recognise paused hand positions rather than the full motion trajectory. This supports my hypothesis that the LSTM's sequential processing misses global gesture patterns
    * Signs that worked consistently: chair, walk, go, help, computer
    * Signs that failed consistently: no, thin, candy, deaf
Key Insight:
The real-world testing confirms that the LSTM architecture struggles with temporal dependencies. When I sign "book" (fold hands then open), the model predicts "help" during the intermediate folding position because it processes frames sequentially without understanding the complete gesture context.
