Project Title: Bidirectional AI-Powered Sign Language Translation System: Cross-Linguistic Transfer Learning for ASL and BSL Recognition

* SUMMARY OF THE PROJECT => I propose developing a bidirectional AI-powered sign language translation system that facilitates real-time communication between deaf/mute individuals and hearing people. The system will support multiple sign languages—primarily British Sign Language (BSL) and American Sign Language (ASL)—to address linguistic diversity within deaf communities. The core technical implementation consists of three integrated components: First, speech-to-text conversion using pre-trained models like OpenAI Whisper, enabling users to speak naturally and have their words converted to text. Second, text-to-sign translation, where the system maps input text to corresponding sign language demonstrations using video datasets (WLASL with 21,000 ASL videos and BSL Corpus with 200+ hours of authentic signing). The system will display these signs either through authentic video playback from the datasets or, if time permits, through a 3D animated avatar that mimics the captured movements using motion capture technology (MediaPipe for hand tracking and pose estimation). Third, sign-to-text recognition using computer vision, where a camera captures the user's hand gestures, MediaPipe extracts hand and body keypoints, and a trained machine learning model (using transfer learning on datasets like WLASL, BSL SignBank) recognizes the signs and converts them to text or speech.

* The system will be delivered as a web-based application with an intuitive interface featuring language selection, real-time bidirectional translation, and controls for playback and review. To ensure practical utility and academic rigor, I will conduct a comprehensive evaluation through both quantitative methods (benchmark testing on standard datasets, measuring recognition accuracy targeting 70-80% for a vocabulary of 500-1,000 signs) and qualitative methods (user studies with 5-10 members of the UK deaf community recruited through organizations like the British Deaf Association). The project addresses a critical accessibility gap, as approximately 466 million people worldwide experience hearing loss, with 151,000 BSL users in the UK alone. Beyond the applied system development, the project offers research contributions including cross-linguistic transfer learning analysis (comparing how well models trained on ASL transfer to BSL, given the different data availability across languages), evaluation of sign language recognition in low-resource scenarios, and documentation of design decisions for building accessible multi-modal AI systems.

* Regarding data availability, I’ve confirmed access to all required datasets. For American Sign Language (ASL), the WLASL dataset includes 21,083 videos covering 2,000 signs, with about 10 examples per sign — more than enough for training and transfer learning. For British Sign Language (BSL), the data is more limited: the BSL Corpus offers 200+ hours of real signing from 249 signers, and BSL SignBank includes around 2,500 documented signs. Unlike WLASL, these aren’t pre-segmented and will need processing to extract individual signs, usually giving only 2–3 examples per sign. Still, this difference in data availability actually supports the research goal — showing how models trained on high-resource ASL can be adapted to lower-resource BSL. My current plan is to focus on around 1,000 ASL signs and 300–500 BSL signs using transfer learning, with realistic accuracy targets of about 75–80% for ASL and 70–75% for BSL, which aligns with previous research benchmarks.
